## 1. Intro

### The Hook

Most portfolios show work. This one shows identity.

Yoesel was not built to impress. It was built to communicate — who this person is, what they stand for, and why their work means something. The project started with a simple question: why do so many creative portfolios feel empty, even when the work inside is strong?

The answer: they present work without a point of view. They list skills without showing soul. They exist, but they do not feel.

Yoesel is the answer to that.

---

Yoesel is built on four core principles:

- **Minimal** — nothing is there without a reason
- **Emotional** — design that makes people feel something
- **Identity-driven** — the work reflects the person, not just the process
- **Artist-focused** — built for people who lead with creativity, not titles

The aesthetic is dark and intentional. Warm amber accents. Quiet motion. Strong typography. Every element is chosen to feel like a creative space, not a corporate page.

## 2. Status Quo

### The Hook

Most creative portfolios fail before the first scroll.

They open with a generic layout. A name in the center. A list of services. A grid of work. And nothing that tells you who this person actually is.

The problem is not the work. The work is often excellent. The problem is the presentation. It feels like a resume, not an identity.

---

### Problem Statement

Typical portfolio websites share the same failures:

- **Generic structure** — header, hero, grid, contact. Every portfolio looks the same
- **Template dependency** — most are built on Squarespace, Wix, or a downloaded theme with minimal customization
- **No point of view** — the designer disappears behind the work
- **Lack of emotional connection** — visitors browse and leave without remembering anything
- **Weak visual identity** — fonts, colors, and spacing are default, not deliberate

The result is a portfolio that is forgettable. Even when the work is great.

---

### Target Audience Problem

Creative artists have a specific problem with their digital presence.

Their work is deeply personal. A musician's identity is tied to sound, feeling, and image. A visual artist's work speaks in mood and form. But most portfolio tools are built for agencies and freelancers, not artists.

The platforms do not understand the need for:

- Emotional tone in design
- Visual hierarchy that reflects personality
- Motion and atmosphere that support the work
- A brand system that is consistent across every page

So artists are left choosing between a generic template that does not fit, or building something from scratch with no guidance.

Most end up with something that does not feel like them.

---

### Design Philosophy

Good design is not decoration. It is communication.

The thinking behind Yoesel is built on one principle: **identity comes before aesthetics**.

Before choosing a color, ask what it means. Before choosing a font, ask what it says. Before adding a section, ask what it does for the person reading it.

This is the opposite of how most portfolios are built. Most people start with a template and try to fit their identity into it. The result is a mismatch — the person feels boxed in.

Yoesel reverses the process. Start with identity. Build the design around it. Let the system reflect the person, not the other way around.

---

### The Real Cost

When a creative portfolio lacks identity, the damage is real:

- Visitors do not remember the person after leaving
- Potential clients cannot form a clear impression
- The work loses context without a strong surrounding identity
- Opportunities are missed because the first impression was forgettable
- The artist loses confidence in their digital presence

A great portfolio does not just show work. It builds trust, memory, and connection in the first few seconds of every visit.

## 6. Design System

### Color Palette

The palette is built on restraint. Two primary tones do all the work.

| Role | Value | Usage |
|---|---|---|
| Background | `#0B0B0F` | Base for all sections |
| Foreground | `rgba(255,255,255,0.88)` | Primary text |
| Accent | `#E6A15A` | Highlights, labels, borders, hover states |
| Muted text | `rgba(255,255,255,0.30–0.45)` | Descriptive copy, secondary info |
| Card surface | `rgba(255,255,255,0.02–0.04)` | Subtle card backgrounds |
| Amber glow | `oklch(0.78 0.12 55)` | Shadows, glows, animated elements |

The accent color appears sparingly. It marks what is important. Labels, section titles, hover states, border highlights. Using it too often would kill its impact.

---

### Typography

All headings use serif. All body and UI text uses sans-serif. The contrast between them creates hierarchy and emotional range.

| Element | Style |
|---|---|
| Display headings | Serif, bold, `clamp(32px–60px)`, letter-spacing `-0.025em` |
| Section labels | Sans-serif, `10–11px`, `0.14–0.16em` tracking, uppercase |
| Body copy | Sans-serif, `13–16px`, `line-height 1.65–1.75` |
| Captions / hints | Sans-serif, `10–12px`, low opacity |

Heading sizes use `clamp()` throughout — they scale smoothly between mobile and desktop without breakpoints.

---

### Spacing System

Spacing follows a consistent clamp-based rhythm tied to viewport width:

```
Tight:    clamp(16px, 2vw, 24px)
Standard: clamp(32px, 4vw, 48px)
Section:  clamp(48px, 7vw, 80px)
Large:    clamp(72px, 10vw, 120px)
```

This means the site breathes differently at different screen sizes, but always in proportion. There are no sudden layout jumps.

---

### Layout Grid

All content uses a shared container:

```css
max-width: 7xl (1280px)
margin: 0 auto
padding: clamp(24px, 4vw, 64px) — horizontal
```

Applied as: `max-w-7xl mx-auto px-6 md:px-10 lg:px-16`

Section grids use CSS Grid with `gap-5` or `gap-6`. Cards use `1fr` columns with responsive breakpoints (`sm:grid-cols-2 md:grid-cols-3`).

---

### UI Consistency

Every interactive element follows three rules:

1. **Hover states are smooth** — `transition: 0.2s–0.3s ease`
2. **Color changes use opacity shifts**, not bright color swaps
3. **Movement is directional** — hover slides elements in the direction of intent (right for links, up for cards)

Border radius: `rounded-2xl` (16px) for cards, `rounded-full` for pills and buttons.

## 7. Interaction Design

### Card Flip — Visual Identity Section

Each logo card in the Visual Identity section has two states:

**Front:** Logo mark and project name. Clean and minimal.

**Back:** Project category, name, short description, and a preview hint.

**Mechanism:**
- CSS `transform-style: preserve-3d` on the card container
- `rotateY(180deg)` on hover (desktop) or tap (mobile)
- Each face uses `backface-visibility: hidden`
- Transition: `0.60s cubic-bezier(0.4, 0, 0.2, 1)`

On desktop, hover flips the card. On touch devices, the first tap flips, the second tap opens the modal.

---

### Modal Preview

Clicking (or double-tapping on mobile) any Visual Identity card opens a full modal:

- Dark overlay with `backdrop-filter: blur(14px)`
- Modal scales in from `0.94 → 1.0` with a simultaneous fade
- Shows large logo, project category, name, and description
- Closes via: X button, clicking outside, or pressing `Escape`
- Body scroll is locked while the modal is open

Animation: `opacity 0.25s ease` for overlay, `cubic-bezier(0.22, 1, 0.36, 1)` for the modal box.

---

### Navigation Interactions

- **Navbar links:** opacity shift on hover, amber underline slides in from left
- **CTA button:** border color and background shift on hover, `translateY(-2px)`
- **Mobile menu:** full-screen overlay, `opacity 0 → 1` fade, `backdrop-filter: blur(20px)`
- **Logo hover:** fill opacity increases on the hexagon polygon

---

### Scroll Reveal

All major sections use a `useReveal` hook built on `IntersectionObserver`:

```
Trigger: element 20% in viewport
Effect: opacity 0 → 1, translateY(40px → 0)
Duration: 0.8s ease-out
Stagger: cards delay by 0.07s × index
```

This creates a natural cascade as content enters the viewport.

---

### Hover States — Cards and Links

All interactive surfaces follow consistent patterns:

- **Work cards:** `translateY(-5px)`, border brightens, box-shadow deepens
- **Skills items:** `translateX(4px)`, label brightens, dot glows
- **Footer nav links:** `padding-left` increases, color shifts to amber
- **Back-to-top button:** arrow `translateY(-3px)`, text brightens

---

### Form Interactions

The contact form uses:

- `border-color` transition to amber on focus
- `box-shadow: 0 0 0 3px rgba(230,161,90,0.08)` on focus
- Submit button: `translateY(-2px)` + deeper shadow on hover
- Success state: animated checkmark with amber glow pulse

## 8. Background System

### Overview

The background is a fixed ambient layer that sits behind all content across every page. It provides a sense of depth and life without competing with the content above it.

The system uses three animated gradient blobs rendered with CSS `@keyframes`, positioned with `position: fixed` and `z-index: -1`.

---

### Architecture

```
html/body → background: #0B0B0F (dark base)
↓
BackgroundLayer (position: fixed, z-index: -1)
  └── base div: background: #0B0B0F
      └── parallax container (moves with mouse)
          ├── Blob 1 (amber, top-right)
          ├── Blob 2 (indigo, bottom-left)
          └── Blob 3 (soft amber, center)
↓
All sections → background: transparent
↓
Content (cards, text, forms) → their own opaque backgrounds
```

All section backgrounds are set to `transparent`, allowing the fixed layer to show through.

---

### Blob Configuration

| Blob | Color | Opacity | Blur | Size | Duration |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 — Amber (top-right) | `rgba(255,140,60,0.18)` | 0.15–0.22 | 72px | 580×520px | 46s |
| 2 — Indigo (bottom-left) | `rgba(80,120,255,0.14)` | 0.11–0.18 | 88px | 520×560px | 62s |
| 3 — Soft amber (center) | `rgba(255,170,90,0.10)` | 0.08–0.12 | 100px | 440×400px | 78s |

Each blob uses a directional radial gradient (`circle at 35% 35%`) to create a bright focal point that fades outward. This gives structure without hard edges.

---

### Movement

Each blob animates between 4 keyframe positions, translating 30–55px and scaling between 0.94 and 1.08. The three blobs use intentionally different durations (46s, 62s, 78s) so they never synchronize — the motion always feels organic, never looping visibly.

Opacity is also animated within each keyframe sequence, creating a slow breathing effect.

---

### Mouse Parallax

A `requestAnimationFrame` loop tracks mouse position and applies a smooth lerp:

- **Target offset:** up to 16px based on mouse position relative to center
- **Lerp factor:** 4% per frame (very smooth catch-up)
- **Effective max movement:** ~8px visible to the user

This creates a subtle sense of depth — the background appears to exist on a slightly different plane from the content.

---

### Performance Decisions

- All blobs use `will-change: transform, opacity` to hint GPU compositing
- `filter: blur()` is applied to fixed-size elements, avoiding reflow
- Mouse tracking uses `{ passive: true }` event listener
- `prefers-reduced-motion` disables all blob animations
- On mobile, animation duration extends to 90s (less perceptible movement, lower battery cost)

### Component Architecture

The site is organized into purpose-built components. No UI library components are used for layout — everything is hand-built.

**Global:**
- `BackgroundLayer` — fixed ambient background (rendered in root layout)
- `Navbar` — fixed top navigation with scroll state
- `Footer` — closing section with brand and bottom bar
- `LoadingScreen` — full-screen intro with logo and progress bar

**Home page sections (in order):**
- `HeroSection`
- `AboutPreviewSection`
- `PortfolioSection`
- `ProcessSection`
- `SkillsSection`
- `LogoSection` (Visual Identity)
- `ContactSection`

**About page sections:**
- Inline hero, bio, core skills, experience timeline, achievements, CTA — built directly in `app/about/page.tsx`

**Shared hooks:**
- `useReveal` — `IntersectionObserver`-based scroll reveal for any element

---

### Reusable Patterns

**Scroll reveal:**
```tsx
const { ref, revealed } = useReveal()
<div ref={ref} className={`reveal ${revealed ? 'revealed' : ''}`}>
```

**Fluid section padding:**
```tsx
paddingTop: 'clamp(48px, 7vw, 80px)'
paddingBottom: 'clamp(48px, 7vw, 80px)'
```

**Hover state (inline, without CSS class):**
```tsx
onMouseEnter={(e) => { e.currentTarget.style.color = '#E6A15A' }}
onMouseLeave={(e) => { e.currentTarget.style.color = 'rgba(255,255,255,0.28)' }}
```

**Hover state (with CSS class, for pseudo-selectors):**
```html
<style>{`.card:hover { transform: translateY(-4px); }`}</style>
```

---

### Responsiveness

The layout uses three strategies in combination:

1. **Tailwind responsive prefixes** — `sm:`, `md:`, `lg:` for column counts and visibility
2. **CSS `clamp()`** — fluid sizing that scales without breakpoints
3. **`flexWrap: 'wrap'` + `flex: '1 1 Xpx'`** — two-column layouts that reflow naturally

No layout uses a fixed pixel width for content containers. Everything adapts to the viewport.

---

### Email API

Contact form submissions go through a Next.js API route (`/api/contact`) using the Resend SDK:

- Validates `name`, `email`, `message` fields on the server
- Sends to `sangayyoesel@gmail.com`
- Returns `{ success: true }` or `{ error: '...' }`
- Form UI handles both success and error states

---

### Performance Optimization

- Images use Next.js `<Image>` with `fill`, `sizes`, and `loading="lazy"` where appropriate
- Background blobs use `will-change` and `filter: blur()` on fixed-size elements
- All animations respect `prefers-reduced-motion`
- Section backgrounds are `transparent` — no redundant paint on top of the fixed background
- `IntersectionObserver` is used for scroll effects instead of scroll event listeners

## 10. User Experience Flow

### First Impression — Loading Screen

The visitor's first experience is the loading screen. It appears before any content and sets the tone for the entire site.

What the visitor sees:
1. The SY hexagon mark — animated with a slow outer ring rotation
2. "YOESEL" in large amber serif — glowing softly
3. A sound-wave bar animation below the name
4. A thin progress bar at the bottom
5. "Loading experience" in small uppercase text

This is not decorative. It establishes the visual language — dark, amber, minimal, identity-forward — before the visitor interacts with a single element.

Duration: approximately 5 seconds. Then a smooth fade transition to the homepage.
