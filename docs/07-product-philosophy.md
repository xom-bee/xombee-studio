## 7.1 Why the website exists

Creative artists are evaluated online by people who will never meet them in person. A
fragmented presence — a handle here, a link-in-bio there — communicates *availability*
but not *identity*. This product exists to give a creative artist a single, owned
place where they are not just **found** but **remembered**. It is the operational form
of the positioning statement:

> I design professional personal websites that help creative artists express their
> identity online.

## 7.2 What problem it solves

| The artist has…                          | The product delivers…                          |
| ---------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- |
| Talent that is felt in person            | An online presence that transmits that feeling |
| Scattered, rented social profiles        | One owned, coherent identity surface           |
| "A link" people glance at and forget     | An experience people remember                  |
| Design that looks like everyone else's   | A felt atmosphere that is theirs                |

The problem is not "they need a website." The problem is **they are seen but not
remembered** — and the site itself is the proof that the problem can be solved.

## 7.3 What makes it different from templates

A template optimizes for *output speed*. This product optimizes for *memory*. The
difference is structural, not cosmetic:

- A template gives you a layout. This gives you an **argument** (the seen/remembered
  thesis) expressed through the experience.
- A template's atmosphere is a theme toggle. This atmosphere is a hand-built canvas
  engine, paced reveals, and a tuned dark palette — **the moat a template cannot
  copy in an afternoon.**
- A template treats accessibility/performance as a checkbox. Here they are part of
  the brand's claim to craft.

The site is itself the portfolio: it demonstrates the exact capability it sells.

## 7.4 Why creative artists are the target audience

Narrowing the audience is a strategic decision, not a limitation:

- Creative artists already understand that **how something feels is the product** —
  they don't need to be convinced that atmosphere matters; they live it.
- Their need ("express identity") aligns exactly with the product's strength
  (atmosphere, narrative, restraint), so the work and the buyer reinforce each other.
- A narrow audience makes every design choice decidable (see the avatar persona in
  [§1.4](01-brand-foundation.md)). Broad audiences produce generic products.

## 7.5 Why emotional storytelling matters

Decisions about people are made emotionally and justified rationally. A hiring
manager, a collaborator, or a fan decides whether an artist is *worth remembering*
in seconds, on feeling. A purely informational site asks the visitor to do the
emotional work themselves; a narrative site does that work *for* them.

That is why the home page leads with a feeling ("are you remembered?") before any
proof, and why the engineering invests in pacing and atmosphere. The thesis and the
medium are the same statement: **memory is the product, and emotion is how memory is
made.**
